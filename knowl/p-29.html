As the following graph illustrates, we put the confidence level \(1-\alpha\) in the center of the t-distribution. Then, since the entire probability represented by the curve must equal 1, a probability of \(\alpha\) must be shared equally among the two "tails" of the distribution. That is, the probability of the left tail is \(\frac{\alpha}{2}\) and the probability of the right tail is \(\frac{\alpha}{2}\text{.}\) If we add up the probabilities of the various parts \((\frac{\alpha}{2} + 1-\alpha + \frac{\alpha}{2})\text{,}\) we get 1. The \(t\)-multiplier, denoted \(t_{\alpha/2}\text{,}\) is the \(t\)-value such that the probability "to the right of it" is \(\frac{\alpha}{2}\text{:}\)
